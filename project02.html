Finding a Hidden Username

If you’ve ever used WhatsApp, Messenger, or iMessage, you’re used to your messages being private and end-to-end. But the internet wasn’t always that way. A long time ago, people chatted using protocols that sent everything including usernames, passwords, and messages, in plain view. No encryption. Nothing. Everything was visible to everyone.


In this article, we’re going to look at a small piece of internet history: a chat system called AIM (AOL Instant Messenger). Our goal is simple: find out what username was used by someone in a recorded chat session.


We have a file, called a “pcap” (short for packet capture). Think of it like a recording of everything that happened on a network for a short period of time. Every time two computers talked to each other, that conversation was saved in this file. Our job is to look through that recording and find the username someone typed when they logged into AIM (AOL Instant Messenger).


Step 1: Open the Recording


We started with a file called evidence01.pcap. Remember the "pcap" file we mentioned above. Think of it like a video recording of everything two computers said to each other over the network. We opened this file in a free tool called Wireshark, a special magnifying glass that lets you look inside network conversations.


Screenshot1


Step 2: Teach Wireshark Where to Look


AIM (AOL Instant Messenger) normally talks through a specific “door” (technical term: port 5190). But in this recording, AIM (AOL Instant Messenger) was using a different door: port 443 (the same door that modern secure websites use). Wireshark didn’t know that so at first, it ignored the AIM traffic on port 443. We fixed this by going into Wireshark’s settings (Preferences - Protocols - AIM) and telling it: “Hey, also check port 443 for AIM traffic.”


Screenshot


Step 3: Find the Username


Once we made that change, Wireshark immediately started labeling the AIM messages correctly. We scrolled through the list of conversations and found packet number 25. In plain English, Wireshark showed:

“AIM Messaging, Outgoing to: Sec558user1”

We clicked on that packet, expanded the details, and there it was, the username Sec558user1, completely visible in plain text. Even the message that was sent was readable.


The Answer: Sec558user1


Why This Matters

This wasn’t just about finding a name. It showed how unencrypted chat works and why it’s dangerous. Everything was wide open: usernames, who you were talking to, even the message itself. No locks, no privacy. That’s why modern apps encrypt everything.
